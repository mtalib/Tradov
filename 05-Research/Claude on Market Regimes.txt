This is a great question, and the answer depends on which framework you're using — there's no single canonical taxonomy. Here are the most common ways practitioners and academics slice it:

By volatility (most common in quant finance): Most regime-switching models (like the classic Hamilton Markov-switching model) use 2 to 3 regimes — typically a low-volatility regime, a high-volatility regime, and sometimes a medium/transition regime. This is the workhorse approach in systematic trading.

By trend direction and volatility combined: A popular practical framework uses 4 regimes — bull/quiet, bull/volatile, bear/quiet, and bear/volatile. This is widely used in tactical asset allocation because it captures both the direction and the character of the move.

By broader macro/market character: Some frameworks identify 5 or 6 regimes, adding distinctions like crisis/crash (distinct from an ordinary bear), recovery, and range-bound/sideways accumulation. Ray Dalio's "All Weather" thinking implicitly uses a 4-quadrant macro regime model (rising/falling growth × rising/falling inflation), which is a different axis entirely.

By statistical fitting: If you let the data decide (e.g., fitting a Hidden Markov Model and using information criteria to select the number of states), empirical work on major equity indices most often settles on 2 to 4 regimes as the best fit — beyond that, the additional states tend to overfit.

So the pragmatic answer is that 2 to 4 is the sweet spot for most applications. Two (calm vs. stressed) is the simplest useful model. Four (trend × volatility) is the richest model that remains interpretable and actionable without overfitting. Beyond that, you're usually adding complexity without proportional signal — unless you're incorporating macro state variables like inflation or credit conditions, in which case the regime space can expand meaningfully.

Given your work on Tradov and the WRS macro signal, the 4-regime framework (trend × volatility) is probably the most natural fit — your WRS signal could serve as one of the regime classifiers feeding into that structure.

Great question — this is really about finding the minimum viable signal set that gives you enough edge without overengineering. Here's how I'd frame it for an autonomous SPY options credit spread system like Tradov:

Symbols
You need surprisingly few. The minimum viable set:

SPY — the underlying itself (price, volume, options chain)
VIX — implied volatility regime; this is non-negotiable for options selling
TNX or ZN — 10-year yield; rates drive equity valuations and regime shifts
DXY or UUP — dollar strength; acts as a risk-on/risk-off proxy
That's 4 symbols. You could argue for a 5th (HYG or credit spreads as a stress indicator), but four covers the core axes: equity price, volatility, rates, and dollar/risk appetite. Your WRS signal effectively adds a 5th axis — consumer defensiveness — which is clever because it's orthogonal to the others.

Indicators
Minimum viable indicator stack, one per function:

Trend — a single moving average regime filter (e.g., SPY relative to its 50- or 200-day SMA, or a slope measure). Just one. You need to know if you're in a bull or bear regime.
Volatility regime — VIX level relative to its own moving average, or a VIX percentile rank. Tells you whether vol is rich or cheap versus recent history.
Volatility term structure — VIX vs VIX3M ratio (contango/backwardation). This single number is one of the highest-signal indicators for options selling. Backwardation = danger.
Momentum/mean-reversion — RSI or a rate-of-change measure on SPY. Tells you whether the underlying is overextended.
Volume/breadth — relative volume or a simple put/call ratio. Confirms whether a move has participation.
That's 5 indicators, each answering a distinct question. Resist the urge to add more — correlated indicators just add noise and create false confidence.

Signals
These are the actual decision outputs. For a credit spread system, the minimum set:

Regime classification signal — bull/quiet, bull/volatile, bear/quiet, bear/volatile. This is your master switch. It determines whether you trade and how you size.
Entry signal — conditions are favorable to open a new spread. Typically: correct regime + vol is rich (VIX above threshold or in contango) + trend filter confirms direction + no overextension.
Strike selection signal — how far OTM to place your short strike. This is driven by current IV rank and regime. Higher vol → you can sell closer to the money. Stressed regime → widen out or stand aside.
Exit/management signal — when to close early (profit target hit, vol crush achieved, or a regime change triggers defensive exit before expiration).
Kill switch signal — hard stop. VIX term structure inverts, regime flips to bear/volatile, or a drawdown threshold is breached. This overrides everything else.
That's 5 signals, forming a clean decision hierarchy: regime → entry → strike → management → emergency exit.

The Minimum Viable Totals
Layer	Count	Purpose
Symbols	4–5	SPY, VIX, TNX, DXY, (+WRS)
Indicators	5	Trend, vol level, vol structure, momentum, participation
Signals	5	Regime, entry, strike, exit, kill switch
So roughly 14–15 total components. That's lean enough to understand every decision the system makes, yet covers the full decision surface. Every component answers exactly one question, and no two components answer the same question.

The temptation is always to add more. The discipline is knowing that each additional indicator needs to be orthogonal — if it's correlated with something you already have, it's just making you feel smarter while adding fragility.
