Mark's 0DTE SPX Options Trading

The video is an interview with **Mark**, a trader described as having moved from construction management into full-time trading. His primary approach is systematic, high-frequency **0DTE SPX options selling**, focused on repeatedly placing small defined-risk trades throughout the trading day rather than trying to predict one perfect market direction. According to the video analysis, he grew an account from roughly **$200,000 to $1.5 million over about two years**, while keeping his largest drawdown under this specific approach to around **8%**.[1]

Mark’s central idea is that he is not primarily trying to forecast the market. Instead, he sees himself first as a **risk manager** and second as a capital allocator. His edge appears to come from scale, repetition, liquidity, defined risk, and structured response to adverse moves. He prefers **SPX** because it is cash-settled, highly liquid, avoids assignment risk, and offers efficient execution for very short-dated index options.[1]

| Area | What he appears to do |
|---|---|
| Main product | **SPX index options**, mostly 0DTE expirations. |
| Main trade type | **Credit spreads**, often managed as individual put and call sides. |
| Frequency | Roughly **100–200 trades per day**, according to the video analysis. |
| Timing | Begins shortly after the open, around **9:32 a.m. Eastern**, then places trades repeatedly throughout the session. |
| Delta zone | Typically sells options around the **7–20 delta** range. |

| Risk posture | Short premium intraday, but paired with longer-dated long-option hedges for tail protection. |

A key phrase from the video analysis is that he wants the portfolio to be **“locally concave for income and globally convex to limit drawdowns.”** In plain English, that means he accepts the normal risk profile of short option premium for daily income, but he tries to protect against extreme market moves using longer-dated long options and strict risk controls.[1]

## Practical strategies you can take from the video

The most useful takeaway is not simply “sell 0DTE options,” but rather to build a **repeatable risk-management framework**. Mark’s approach depends on many trades, consistent sizing, defined risk, and discipline. For most traders, copying the trade frequency exactly would be difficult and risky, so the safer lesson is to adapt the structure rather than the scale.

| Strategy principle | How you could apply it carefully |
|---|---|
| Use defined-risk structures | Prefer vertical spreads or iron condors over naked short options, especially with 0DTE options. |
| Trade liquid products only | SPX is attractive because of liquidity and cash settlement, but execution quality still matters. |
| Avoid high-risk event days | Consider avoiding FOMC days, CPI releases, major macro events, or days with unusually high expected movement. |
| Size small and repeatable | Use position sizing that allows you to survive several losses without emotional or account-level damage. |
| Predefine risk exits | Decide before entry when a spread will be closed, rolled, or left alone. |
| Use hedges deliberately | If selling short-dated premium, consider whether longer-dated long options are needed to protect against tail events. |
| Track performance statistically | This type of strategy should be evaluated across many trades, not based on one winning or losing day. |
| Reduce emotional triggers | His grayscale-screen habit is unusual, but the principle is useful: remove visual or emotional cues that lead to impulsive decisions. |

## 0DTE SPX strategies he uses or discusses

The following are the specific **0DTE SPX-related strategies and structures** identified from the video analysis.

| Strategy | Description | How he uses it |
|---|---|---|
| **0DTE SPX Credit Spreads / Verticals** | Selling a short option and buying a further OTM option on the same side, same expiration. | This is his core daily income engine. He sells many small defined-risk spreads throughout the day. |
| **Put Credit Spreads** | Bullish or neutral short-premium structure using puts below the market. | Used when selling downside premium, often in the 7–20 delta area. |
| **Call Credit Spreads** | Bearish or neutral short-premium structure using calls above the market. | Used when selling upside premium, often paired with put spreads to form an iron-condor-like book. |
| **Iron Condors** | Combination of a put credit spread and a call credit spread. | He may view the combined book as an iron condor, but manages each side individually. |
| **Synthetic Naked Credit Spreads** | Very wide or cheap-wing credit spreads where the long option mainly caps risk and improves margin efficiency. | He buys far OTM wings, making the trade defined-risk while behaving somewhat like a naked short option position. |
| **Rolling Threatened Short Legs** | Adjusting a spread when the short strike is challenged. | If one side of an iron-condor-like position is threatened, he may roll the short leg while keeping the long wing. |
| **Long Tail Hedges** | Longer-dated long SPX options, usually 1 day to 1 week out. | Used to create convex protection against large moves and offset short 0DTE gamma risk. |
| **Calendarized / Vega-Oriented Structures** | Structures involving different expirations to capture or manage volatility exposure. | Mentioned as an occasional tool to gain a vega edge rather than his core 0DTE engine. |

## Important caution

0DTE SPX strategies can produce fast losses because **gamma risk increases sharply near expiration**. Mark’s approach appears to rely on professional-level discipline, substantial trade frequency, strong execution, and strict risk management. Treat the video as an educational case study rather than a simple template to copy. If you test any version of these ideas, it would be prudent to start in simulation or with very small defined-risk positions.
